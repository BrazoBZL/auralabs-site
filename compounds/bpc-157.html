Overview

BPC-157, or Body Protection Compound 157, is a synthetic pentadecapeptide — a chain of 15 amino acids — derived from a partial sequence of a naturally occurring protein isolated from human gastric juice. It was first characterized in the early 1990s by researchers at the University of Zagreb, led by Dr. Predrag Sikirić, whose laboratory has since produced a substantial body of preclinical literature on the compound.

The compound has attracted sustained research attention largely because of its apparent stability in gastric environments and the breadth of biological contexts in which it has demonstrated activity in animal models. Unlike many peptides, BPC-157 appears to retain structure under physiological conditions that would rapidly degrade similar sequences.

Research has investigated BPC-157 in models of soft tissue injury, gastrointestinal pathology, neurological damage, and systemic healing responses. The compound is notable in the research literature for the diversity of systems in which biological activity has been reported, which has made it a subject of ongoing mechanistic investigation.

Mechanism of Action

The precise mechanisms underlying BPC-157's reported biological activity are not fully characterized, and research is ongoing. However, several pathways have been identified as candidates in the literature.

Preclinical studies have reported that BPC-157 appears to promote angiogenesis — the formation of new blood vessels — through pathways involving vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). Researchers have proposed that upregulation of VEGF receptor expression may partially explain the accelerated vascularization observed in animal wound-healing models.

BPC-157 has also been studied for its apparent interaction with the nitric oxide (NO) system. Some research suggests that modulation of NO signaling may contribute to the compound's reported effects on both gastrointestinal mucosal integrity and vascular function. The compound appears to influence eNOS (endothelial nitric oxide synthase) activity in certain experimental contexts.

In tendon and ligament models, researchers have observed that BPC-157 appears to accelerate fibroblast migration and proliferation, potentially through activation of FAK-paxillin signaling pathways — which are involved in cell adhesion and directional movement. This has been proposed as a contributing mechanism in connective tissue repair studies.

Primary Pathways Under Investigation
  • VEGF / angiogenesis signaling
  • Nitric oxide (eNOS) modulation
  • FAK-paxillin pathway (fibroblast migration)
  • Growth factor receptor interactions
  • Gastrointestinal mucosal cytoprotection
  • Dopaminergic system interactions

Storage & Reconstitution

Storage Conditions
−20°C Long-Term Store lyophilized BPC-157 at −20°C in a dry, dark environment. Avoid repeated freeze-thaw cycles, which can degrade peptide integrity over time.
Temperature Lyophilized: −20°C (stable up to 2 years). Reconstituted: 2–8°C, use within 4–6 weeks.
Light Protection Keep in opaque or amber vial. UV exposure degrades peptide bonds over extended periods.
Reconstitution Overview
  1. Allow vial to reach room temperature before opening to prevent condensation contamination.
  2. Use bacteriostatic water (BAC water) or sterile water for injection as the reconstitution solvent.
  3. Inject solvent slowly along the vial wall — do not inject directly onto the lyophilized powder.
  4. Gently swirl the vial to dissolve. Do not shake or vortex.
  5. Once reconstituted, store at 2–8°C and use within 4–6 weeks for optimal stability.
The reconstitution guidance above is provided for informational purposes related to laboratory handling. It does not constitute a protocol recommendation. All handling should follow institutional laboratory standards.
