# Quality Assessment Guide for Peptide Research Materials

## Introduction

Peptide quality directly impacts research validity. A contaminated, impure, or incorrectly synthesized peptide can skew results, waste months of work, and compromise institutional credibility. This guide provides researchers with practical frameworks to evaluate peptide purity, authenticity, and batch consistency before purchase.

## 1. Understanding Purity Standards

### HPLC Analysis (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography)

**What it measures:** The percentage of your target peptide versus degradation products and impurities in a sample.

**Industry Standards:**
- Research-grade: ≥95% purity
- Pharmaceutical-grade: ≥99% purity
- Aura Labs minimum: 98% (verified by third-party HPLC)

**How to read an HPLC report:**
- Main peak = your compound (should be ≥98% of total peak area)
- Smaller peaks = degradation products, synthesis byproducts
- Baseline noise = solvent/trace contaminants
- Retention time = fingerprint (must match reference standard)

**Red flags:**
- Purity listed as "approximately 95%" (vague, not tested)
- No HPLC report provided
- HPLC peaks that don't match compound structure
- Multiple unexplained peaks >2% each

### Mass Spectrometry (MS)

**What it measures:** Molecular weight confirms you have the correct compound, not a similar molecule.

**Key indicators:**
- M+ ion (molecular ion peak) should match expected mass ±0.5 Da
- Isotope pattern should match theoretical distribution
- Fragment patterns are unique to each peptide

**Why it matters:** Two peptides can look similar on HPLC but have different amino acid sequences. MS confirms molecular identity.

### Water Content (Karl Fischer Titration)

**What it measures:** How much water is absorbed in your lyophilized peptide (moisture).

**Acceptable range:**
- Lyophilized peptides: ≤5% water content
- Stored peptides degrade if water content exceeds 8%

**Why it matters:** Peptides with high water content degrade faster, reducing shelf life and potency.

**Red flags:**
- No water content specification
- Water content >6% for lyophilized material

## 2. Certificate of Analysis (CoA) — What to Look For

A proper CoA should include:

### Essential Components

| Component | What to Check |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Batch Number** | Unique identifier linking to test results |
| **Synthesis Date** | Should be recent (<3 months old is ideal) |
| **HPLC Purity** | ≥95% minimum; ≥98% preferred |
| **Molecular Weight** | Must match theoretical mass ±0.5 Da |
| **Water Content** | ≤5% for lyophilized peptides |
| **Endotoxin Testing** | ≤10 EU/mg for injectable-grade applications |
| **Sterility** | Pass/Fail (if applicable) |
| **Storage Conditions** | -20°C ±5°C or -80°C ±5°C recommended |

### Red Flags in CoA Documentation

- Missing HPLC data (purity unverified)
- No batch number or synthesis date
- Water content unlisted
- Generic specifications ("standard quality")
- Abbreviations without explanation
- Unsigned or undated report
- No laboratory accreditation (ISO 17025, GLP certification)

### Lab Accreditation to Verify

Ask your supplier: "Is your testing lab ISO 17025 accredited?"

**ISO 17025** = Verified competence in testing and calibration. This is the gold standard for analytical labs.

**GLP (Good Laboratory Practice)** = Regulatory compliance (FDA, EPA approved).

Without accreditation, test results may not be defensible in peer review or regulatory submission.

## 3. Peptide Degradation & Stability

### Common Degradation Pathways

**Oxidation**
- Methionine residues oxidize to methionine sulfoxide
- Tryptophan oxidizes to kynurenine
- Visible sign: Yellow/brown discoloration
- Prevention: Store in inert atmosphere (nitrogen), away from light

**Hydrolysis**
- Aspartic acid and glutamic acid bonds break down
- Peptide chain fragments
- Visible sign: Solution becomes cloudy or precipitate forms
- Prevention: Store at -20°C or colder, keep dry

**Deamidation**
- Asparagine (N) and glutamine (Q) residues lose ammonia
- Changes charge and structure
- Not visible; requires MS to detect
- Prevention: Store at -20°C, maintain dry conditions

### Storage Stability Checklist

| Factor | Optimal | Acceptable | Avoid |
|--------|---------|-----------|-------|
| **Temperature** | -80°C | -20°C ±5°C | >4°C |
| **Humidity** | <10% RH | <20% RH | >30% RH |
| **Light** | Dark/opaque | Amber vial | Clear vial |
| **Atmosphere** | Nitrogen | Sealed | Exposed |
| **pH (if liquid)** | 6.5-7.5 | 6.0-8.0 | <5 or >9 |

**Shelf life expectations:**
- Lyophilized, -20°C: 2-3 years
- Lyophilized, -80°C: 5+ years
- Liquid solution, -20°C: 6-12 months
- Liquid solution, 4°C: 2-4 weeks

## 4. Batch Consistency & Testing Reproducibility

### Why Batch Consistency Matters

Different batches of the same peptide can have:
- Slightly different purity (95% vs 98%)
- Different water content
- Different degradation profiles
- Inconsistent biological activity

If your research compares batches, standardize on a single batch or document batch differences.

### Requesting Multiple Batch Tests

**For critical research:**
1. Ask supplier for HPLC analysis of the exact batch before purchase
2. Compare water content across batches (should be similar)
3. Request MS confirmation of molecular weight
4. Test shelf-life degradation (how quickly it degrades over months)

**Cost:** Extra $100-300 for batch verification, but prevents research failure.

### Reproducibility Checklist

- [ ] Same batch number across all experiments
- [ ] CoA matches received batch (verify batch number on container)
- [ ] Store identically (same -20°C freezer, same vial type)
- [ ] Use within 6 months of receipt
- [ ] Document any changes (color, precipitation, odor)

## 5. Red Flags: Identifying Low-Quality Suppliers

### Supplier-Level Red Flags

| Red Flag | What It Means |
|----------|--------------|
| No CoA provided | Unverified quality |
| CoA lacks HPLC data | Purity not tested |
| Extremely low price (<30% below market) | Possible contamination, degradation, or misrepresentation |
| No batch number tracking | Can't reproduce results |
| Supplier refuses batch verification | Quality control issues |
| No third-party testing | Lab may lack credibility |
| Inconsistent product (looks different between orders) | Manufacturing inconsistency |
| Long lead times unexplained | Storage/handling issues |

### Questions to Ask Before Purchase

1. "Can you provide HPLC analysis of the exact batch I'm purchasing?"
2. "Is your testing lab ISO 17025 accredited?"
3. "What is the water content of this lyophilized peptide?"
4. "How is this peptide stored, and what is shelf-life at -20°C?"
5. "Can you provide batch test history (previous batches tested)?"
6. "Do you test for endotoxins?"
7. "What is your return policy if batch testing fails?"

**If supplier can't answer these clearly:** Find another supplier.

## 6. Practical Quality Assessment Workflow

### Before Purchase

```
1. Request CoA for exact batch
   ↓
2. Verify HPLC purity ≥95% (≥98% preferred)
   ↓
3. Check water content ≤5%
   ↓
4. Confirm lab accreditation (ISO 17025)
   ↓
5. Verify storage conditions match your needs
   ↓
6. Compare to competitors (price + quality)
   ↓
7. Purchase + document batch number
```

### Upon Receipt

```
1. Open in controlled environment (minimize light/moisture exposure)
   ↓
2. Verify batch number matches CoA
   ↓
3. Inspect visually (color, precipitation, crystallization)
   ↓
4. Document appearance + odor
   ↓
5. Store immediately at -20°C or -80°C
   ↓
6. Archive CoA with batch number + receipt date
```

### During Experiments

```
1. Use same batch for entire experiment
   ↓
2. Thaw on ice (minimize thermal stress)
   ↓
3. Minimize exposure to light/oxygen
   ↓
4. If multi-batch comparison, document batch details
   ↓
5. Report batch number in Methods section
```

## 8. Troubleshooting: What If Quality Is Questionable?

### If You Receive Degraded Product

**Signs:**
- Color change (yellow, brown, or dark discoloration)
- Precipitation or cloudiness
- Unusual odor
- Crystallization patterns that don't match storage conditions

**Action:**
1. Do NOT use in experiments
2. Photograph the sample
3. Contact supplier with photos + batch number
4. Request replacement + refund
5. Store original sample for investigation

### If HPLC Results Don't Match CoA

1. Request re-testing from independent lab
2. Compare to reference standard (if available)
3. Verify batch number (ensure correct batch was tested)
4. Document discrepancies

### If Shelf Life Is Shorter Than Expected

1. Check storage conditions (temperature, humidity, light exposure)
2. Compare water content at purchase vs. current (may indicate moisture absorption)
3. Re-test with MS to identify degradation products
4. Adjust storage protocol (move to -80°C instead of -20°C)

## Conclusion

Quality assessment is not optional — it's the foundation of reproducible science. By requesting CoAs, verifying purity, checking accreditation, and documenting batch details, you protect your research and your institutional credibility.

**Three-step summary:**
1. Before purchase: Request HPLC + CoA verification
2. Upon receipt: Verify batch number + storage conditions
3. In experiments: Use same batch, report batch details

Quality matters. Choose suppliers who prove it.
